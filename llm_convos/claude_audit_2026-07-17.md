## 2. Overall assessment

The experiment phase is the part of a project like this that usually breaks quietly, and it did not break quietly here: it broke loudly, twice, and the discipline held both times. A fatal CPython evaluator state killed attempt 1 after fitting but before any raw result; the registered unread-infrastructure exception was applied exactly within its scope (artifact existence, counts, and whole-file hashes only — no metric read); the author stopped attempt 2 on a time budget; the repair became a separately versioned execution-only amendment with bitwise differential tests rather than a silent patch; and the confirmation then ran once, with every stage hash recorded. The identical model-archive hash across CPython 3.13.3 and 3.13.14 is quiet but valuable determinism evidence. The adjudication of the result is honest to the point of severity: the pre-checkpoint note's insistence that "mixed" means *decisive effects in opposite directions*, not sampling ambiguity, is exactly right and must survive into the paper.

This audit's two headline findings are of different kinds, and neither touches the internal validity of the run.

**First (C20):** the repository's public verifiability is currently broken by a line-ending transport defect. Every committed frozen JSON artifact fails its recorded SHA-256 on a fresh clone; restoring CRLF line endings recovers every recorded hash exactly, on all six files I tested; the cause is Windows text-mode newline translation at artifact-write time colliding with git's LF normalization at commit time; and the public CI has been red for six consecutive commits — from the Task 19A freeze through HEAD — while four completion notes state that all tests pass. The cause is completely benign, the run's internal chain is intact, and the fix costs an hour. But a project whose entire evidential architecture is "run one command and check the hashes" cannot ship with that command failing, and the fact that the red public signal went unremarked through four task completions is a process gap worth closing permanently (S13).

**Second (C21):** the boundary-panel half of `F35`'s registered prediction was inconsistent with the corpus's own theory at freeze time, in a way derivable from the frozen constants in one page. The cleanest layer: `F36` demands 90% marginal target-in-proposal coverage, which forces the accepted envelope's upper endpoint above the oracle region's upper endpoint almost always — and the boundary panel's exact-equality cell defines Supported as `ε = u*` precisely. Satisfying `F36` therefore *mechanically* forfeits that cell to the conservative decoder, while an unconstrained classifier just learns the label prior. The registered conjunction contained a structural tension. The deeper layer extends this to the strict-near cells via the hidden-intercept arithmetic (§5, C21). The consequence is a reframing, not a retraction: the "mixed" result is to a large extent *the geometry of conservative decoding, empirically quantified* — which is a better paper sentence than "surprising trade-off," and it converts the required future-work item from "tune the loss" to "change the decision geometry."

Beyond those two, the phase is coherent, cumulative, and faithful to the author's guidance, and the founding intuitions come out of the experiment in a strikingly asymmetric way that the paper should exploit (§8).

## 5. Corrections (C20–C22)

**C20 — Line-ending transport breaks every public hash equation; CI has been red since the freeze; repair is cheap and should precede any further commit.** The facts, each verified: (i) `python -m verification` on a fresh Linux clone fails 3 of 161 tests — the v1 preflight, the v1.1 contract check, and the protocol/pilot self-consistency test — all with hash mismatches. (ii) The committed `protocol_v1.json` hashes to `6e48f4…` while `implementation_v1.json`, `implementation_v1_1.json`, and `analysis_v1_1.json` all expect `3467f5e2…`; the committed `manifests_v1.json`, `pilot_results_v1.json`, and all four stage checkpoints likewise fail their recorded values. (iii) Converting each committed file's LF line endings to CRLF recovers the recorded hash **exactly, in all six cases I tested** — so the artifacts are byte-identical to the frozen originals modulo an information-free newline transform, and there is no content drift anywhere. (iv) The causal story is complete: the recorded `python_runtime` is Windows; `_atomic_json` writes via text-mode `write_text`, which translates `\n` to `\r\n` on Windows; the runners hashed those CRLF bytes; git normalized to LF at commit. The `.py` source hashes pass because the sources were authored LF — which is also why the failure is confined to generated artifacts. (v) The GitHub Actions history shows `Verify finite witness` red for exactly the six commits from `6993c40` (Task 19A) through `93e3ce8`, and green before. Four completion notes in that window assert that all repository tests pass; that was true on the author's working tree and false for the repository as published.

Repair, in order: (a) add a `.gitattributes` declaring the frozen artifacts exempt from EOL normalization (e.g. `experiments/*.json -text`) and recommit the byte-exact CRLF originals so every recorded hash verifies from a clean clone — this preserves the frozen bytes rather than re-freezing anything; (b) pass `newline="\n"` in `_atomic_json` (and any sibling writers) so future artifacts are platform-invariant at the source; (c) record the whole event as an **execution-environment erratum**, not a protocol deviation — the run's internal verification chain was self-consistent throughout, and nothing scientific changes; (d) see S13 for the process guard, and L10 for the durable convention. Severity: high for external auditability (this is the one defect in the repository that *looks* like tampering to a third party running the advertised check), zero for internal validity, and near-zero to fix.

**C21 — The boundary endpoint's registered direction contradicted the corpus's own theory, derivably, at freeze time.** Two layers. *Layer one needs no arithmetic:* the boundary panel's exact-equality cell labels `ε = u*` Supported (inclusive convention), while decoding Supported requires `upper(U_safe) ≤ ε`. `F36` requires the accepted envelope to contain the sampled target at 90% marginally, which places `upper(U_safe)` above `u*` with high probability by construction. So the two registered hypotheses were in structural tension: `F36` succeeding (it did) near-mechanically forfeits that cell (it was forfeited), while direct CE simply learns the label prior there. *Layer two prices the strict-near cells:* the generator's hidden world intercepts `b_J ~ N(0, .006²)` against `σ_J ∈ [.006, .014]` are invisible to any permitted scorer input, so they are irreducible center uncertainty of roughly `0.4–1.0σ`; covering the target at 90% then forces an accepted half-width of about `1.645·√(σ²+σ_b²) + η ≈` the oracle half-width plus `≳ .27σ` — larger than the *entire* `.25σ` window that defines the boundary panel. Every supported/refuted case inside that window decodes Open, exactly as the corpus's own Theorem 2 (conservative recovery guaranteed only outside the band) says it must. The observed numbers confirm the model quantitatively: accepted width `.0401` versus oracle `≈ .033` for `J`; support-miss `.4611` against my back-of-envelope `≈ .5` from the stratum mix; structured boundary accuracy `.5196` ≈ near-perfect crossing-open plus heavily suppressed remaining cells. And the calibration pattern is diagnostic: the frozen planning alternatives were *almost exactly right* on transfer (planned `.90/.80`; observed `.9436/.7570`) and wrong precisely where they contradicted the theory (planned structured `.90` on boundary; observed `.5196`). The variance planning was properly conservative; the effect-*direction* assumptions were never derived at all.

Consequences: (a) Checkpoint C1 and the paper should present the boundary and in-regime deficits primarily as *predicted-in-hindsight structure* — the dead-band cost of conservative decoding under irreducible latent heterogeneity — with the experiment supplying the quantification; this is more honest and more interesting than "unexpected mixed result," and it makes the transfer win (which was *not* similarly forced: CE received the tolerance as an input and was free to extrapolate it) the genuinely informative component. (b) Add one standing rule to the preregistration pattern: before any protocol freezes, attempt to **derive each registered directional alternative from the frozen generator constants plus the corpus's own theorems**, and either reconcile discrepancies or redesign the endpoint. This is a one-page exercise that would have caught both layers here, and — unlike fresh-eyes review — it does not depend on who is in the context window (see §7 on the compressed 07-14 evening). (c) No retroactive relabeling: the frozen rule was applied correctly, `I1` stands, and none of this is a rescue.

**C22 — Checkpoint C's "at matched coverage" qualifier did not survive into the frozen primary, and its loss explains part of C21's tilt.** Checkpoint C §4.6 specified "tolerance-transfer fidelity, false support/refutation, and boundary risk **at matched coverage**" as common endpoints. The frozen `E_boundary` is raw macro accuracy — no coverage matching anywhere in the primaries — which conflates the information quality of the two arms with their (radically different) abstention policies: an arm at `0.9962` fallback and an arm at `0.9139` are not answering the same question when scored on raw accuracy. The frozen rule governs and nothing is invalidated; this is a traceable drift between checkpoint guidance and preregistration, worth recording as such at C1. Forward obligations: any separately versioned follow-up on the conservatism mechanism should make matched-coverage comparison (or full risk–coverage curves, which the corpus already owns via its selective-prediction sources) the *primary* analysis; and the paper's presentation of the boundary/in-regime results must keep fallback mass and miss rates physically adjacent to the accuracy numbers, as the C1 note already recommends.

## 7. Difficulty calibration, failures, successes, and discoveries

- **Harder than predicted: running the experiment at all.** The last audit's worry was statistical (an omnibus too broad to power its core); the actual near-fatal risk was engineering — a CPython 3.13 evaluator defect surfaced by 33.6M redundant object audits, plus the raw cost of an object-per-atom design at 40,000 world-seed evaluations. The recovery is a model of how to fail: unread-failure exception applied within scope, author time-budget stop recorded as such, versioned execution-only amendment with bitwise equivalence gates, and a decision-log entry ("high-volume semantics must be data-oriented, with C++ as the custom-kernel fallback") that converts the incident into standing policy. Worth noting for the paper's reproducibility appendix: the identical model-archive hash across two interpreter versions.
- **As predicted: the S11 discipline carried the phase.** The minimum core stayed minimal, the embargo held through two failures, hard MoE died at its own prospective gate rather than by drift, and the one confirmation run was boring — which was the design goal.
- **A tempo observation, not a violation.** Preregistration, pilot/freeze, and implementation all landed within roughly seventy minutes on the evening of 07-14. The load-bearing property of preregistration — design fixed before any outcome contact — demonstrably held: final worlds were never materialized until 07-16 and the guarded entry point enforced it. What the compression does cost is independence between the design and the pilot that "validates" it, since both share one context. The context-independent mitigation is exactly C21's derivation rule: a check that runs on paper against frozen constants does not care who is performing it or when.
- **Failures: none silent.** The v1 runner (documented in three artifacts), the boundary misprediction (surfaced here as C21 — a specification-side failure, the same genus as my own S6 error at Checkpoint B, and worth recording in the same register), and the CRLF transport defect (C20).
- **Discoveries worth surfacing.** (i) The transfer/conservatism trade-off *with its structural explanation* — the dead band as the price of coverage — which is a genuinely reusable design fact about certificate-based neural interfaces. (ii) `F36` passing cleanly: the proposal-binding/calibration/checker machinery works as specified end-to-end, which is the quiet prerequisite for everything the paper wants to say about accepted evidence. (iii) Fallback mass `0.9962` as the operational headline: the license economy was representable and calibratable but, as configured, almost never grants — the sharpest available statement that sound withholding and useful coverage are different objectives. (iv) The design-alternative calibration pattern (right where theory-aligned, wrong where theory-contradicted) as meta-evidence for C21's rule. (v) For the methods literature the project cites: a live demonstration that a preregistered conjunction can be decisively informative in both directions at once, provided the reverse rule was also frozen.

**The founding intuitions, read against the result.** This is the checkpoint's most important interpretive fact, and the C1 note gets it right without quite saying it this way: **the experiment's winning component is the founding intuition, and its losing component is not.** The transfer result — a numerical region reused, without retraining, under a changed tolerance, at `0.9436` — is the direct empirical cash value of `claude.txt`'s central image: ReLU as margin, "the unspent tolerance," "licenses become resource-valued, and downstream units consume slack as evidence," and of `chatgpt.txt`'s positive-adequacy-margin gate with tied `log ε` dependence. What lost — the operational classifier default — is a Task 18 engineering choice the founding materials never asserted; indeed `claude.txt` itself flagged the cost in advance ("at the cost of erasing defeater strength: 'barely inadequate' and 'wildly inadequate' both transmit zero"), and the abstention requirement ("an honest atlas must be able to output… no chart") is honored by the structured arm at a frequency the intuition simply never priced. So the damage assessment for the author's actual concerns is asymmetric in the project's favor, and the paper should say so with the specific lineage: *the margin-as-reusable-resource intuition survived its first adversarial empirical test; the untested add-on was the claim that the certificate pipeline would also be the better classifier.* One founding sentence does need a permanent qualifier: the `claude.txt` coda "gradient descent built the license calculus" can no longer be quoted straight — this experiment shows gradient descent building a license interface that is *calibratable and safe but nearly non-granting*; recognition is not yet operational competitiveness. The posts' recursive-judgment and policy/value intuitions are untouched by `F35` and correctly remain gated behind Tasks 22–22A.

**Trajectory to the consumable products.** Paradoxically improved. A clean `F35` win would have handed Task 25 a smooth story and a referee-shaped vulnerability; what it has instead is a real tension with a structural explanation, a supported registered sub-result, a passing calibration claim, and a limitation (coverage) that connects the paper to an established literature. The lead question — *why this value-logic design, and why hope it fits neural networks?* — now has its empirical paragraph determined: the design's information-preservation half was vindicated exactly where the theory located it (tolerance change), and its decision-quality half was refuted exactly where the theory, properly consulted, located that too (inside the band). That is a paper with a spine. The remaining trajectory risks are unchanged from audit III — register inversion at Task 25/31A — plus the small new one that the trade-off gets narrated as mystery rather than geometry, which C21's framing prevents.
